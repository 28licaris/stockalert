## SECTION 10: LESSONS FROM JUNE 18–26 SESSION

### 1. Analyst Count Changes Override Prior Trade Thesis

**Example:** On June 11–12, Avi shifted from a "1-2 setup" to a confirmed "(i)-(ii)-(iii) complete, (iv) forming" count.

**Lesson:** When Avi publishes an update, re-align immediately. The prior bias (short-term vs. long-term) becomes secondary to the new count structure. Shane **correctly** abandoned the initial bounce-short thesis (which was looking for the top of wave iv at 4220) and flipped to waiting for the full bounce into 4,267–4,340.

### 2. Don't Chase the Hole; Wait for the Bounce to Fail

**Example:** Price dropped from 4,391 to 4,160 (–231 pts). This was impulsive, the natural place to short. But the rule is: **don't short the bottom of a vertical drop; short the bounce that follows.**

**Lesson:** Entered long at 4,180.7 on the bounce, not at 4,160. When the bounce failed at 4,212, exited with minimal loss. This is better than chasing 4,160 with RSI at 30.

### 3. Truncation ≠ Count Failure

**Example:** Wave v of the (c)-wave is expected to reach 3,735–3,565. If it only reaches 3,900, that's a **truncation** (a valid pattern where wave v fails to exceed wave iii), not a broken count. The (c)-wave still completes; it's just weaker than expected.

**Lesson:** A truncated fifth still requires all prior waves (i–iv) to be correctly labeled and valid. It's a rarer outcome, but it doesn't invalidate the Elliott Wave count — it just signals weakness and a potential sharp reversal (up) after the low.

### 4. Add Into Pullbacks, Not Spikes

**Example:** During the bounce to 4,239, Shane added a third contract near the high (4,226) instead of on the dips. This pulled the average up and forced a tight stop, which got run through on a wick.

**Lesson:** Add into higher-lows (pullbacks within the bounce) to preserve stop room. Adding into the spike compresses risk and leads to premature stopouts on noise.

### 5. Wave 4 Bounces Are Counter-Trend; Don't Marry Them

**Example:** The bounce from 4,046 to 4,391 looked like it could go higher. But Avi's count is clear: it's a corrective (iv) bounce, not the start of a new impulse. Once it failed at 4,391, the count was reconfirmed.

**Lesson:** Even if a bounce feels "strong," if the larger count says it's corrective (wave 2 or 4 in an impulse), scale out at resistance and don't hold for the next big target. The real trade is the wave (v) decline after the bounce fails.

### 6. Invalidation Levels Are Not "Soft"

**Example:** Avi set 4,402 as the hard line above which the purple (bullish) count activates. Price touched 4,391 and rolled over. This **confirmed** the primary bearish count was still valid.

**Lesson:** Invalidation levels are a binary gate. If price exceeds them with structure, the count is wrong and the plan changes. If price tests but rejects them, the count holds. Don't treat invalidation levels as "soft" or "it's okay if we go a little past them" — the rules are the rules.
